Hello family and friends, I bring a topic of technology. I'm hoping to hear your voices on this. This is my (hand written) adventure with AssessingAgents, and where I think it can reach. 

AssessingAgents is a series of instructions ran by computer agents. To appraise and exempt properties. Just as a real Assessor's Office would do. 

AssessingAgents achieves excellent results on small very clean simulated data. I think it can do the same for real data. 

AssessingAgents is open source. Meant for everyday assessors. People who want to see competing tax rolls. And choose the best. 

This is my contribution to equality. I hope to impact property assessment. Fairer tax rolls for all. 

## In more detail 

It all started working at the Cook County Assessor's Office, CCAO. I hold a great memory of it. The CCAO is modern and transparent Assessor's office. And even they show errors in fairness. Their tax roll is one of the best, and still regressive (CCAO fails in the exemption process, not in appraisal, see more at https://www.cookcountyassessoril.gov/news/assessors-office-releases-2024-chicago-reassessment-report).

Owners of inexpensive property carry the load. They pay more taxes in percentage. 

The CCAO is relatively large. And it has a state of the art workflow. 

Here is my hipothesis. I believe small, low budget Assessor's Office don't have state of the art workflows.

This is painful. They are too slow in producing tax rolls. And decisions are obscure.  

They could use cheap, open source software. That can produce n tax rolls for m money.

Assessors need to pick and choose the best tax roll. AssesingAgents wants to put tax rolls in a coliseum. And highlight best qualities.

I think assessors are willing to pay for it. Albeit not all their budget. 

So now I look to connect. With assessors who want to try agentic infrastrucutre. Or AssessingAgents in general. I look to learn about their needs.  

There are plnety of small Assessors'. MA has around 350. Many potential clients. 

Finally, there is no open source solution. I think it's a great time for it. 

## Going deeper

AssessingAgents offers appraisal and exemption. You just feed it data. AssessingAgents looks at the data. describes a plan, runs calculations, and outputs a tax roll. Focusing on fairness and client notes. 

I have produced excellentlly fair tax rolls, and demonstrated guardrails. By running AssessingAgents on Tralaland. A well describe quadrant of 20 properties. Feed good data to AssessingAgents and you will get a fair tax roll. 

Under the hood we have 6 Forge agents doing tasks sequantially. See the repo at https://github.com/TristanGramsch/AssessingAgents. 

Languages play a big role, I believe. The more the better. 

Tralaland can be expressed in standard languages. .gml (including citygml), .geojason, and .ifc in the future. 

There is no standard for social conditions. That's a challenge. Ownership and sale history is a .yalm. 

## More research

In my effort to understand the world of assessment I stumbled into .ifc and into regressivity in Allston. .ifc is a file format representing the Industry Foundation Classes. Allston is where I live. 

.ifc is probably the most detailed standard way to describe a property. Providing standard pricing. Can assessors use it?

Below, an image of an .ifc 3d model of my house (abstract as you can note). Easy to make. 

I also stumbled and I tried fixing the (slightly) regressive assessment in Allston.

I downloaded and parsed real assessment data of Boston. Sales of 2024, and assessed values of 2025. I filtered for Allston. 
I removed Boston's official assessment values (most importantly TOTAL_VALUE of the property). And calculated my own in a clean slate. I inferred an almost flat tax rate (but this step can be simplified). 

Then I (with gpt) measured whether properties with higher sale prices tend to have lower (or higher) effective tax rate. corr(log(sale_price), effective_tax_rate)

A negative correlation means that as log(sale_price) increases (i.e., as sale price gets larger),the effective tax rate tends to be lower on average.

REAL assessor data:
corr(log(sale_price), effective_tax_rate): -0.03550
Slightly regressive. 

My version (Ridge regression predicted total value and recomputed tax):
corr(log(sale_price), effective_tax_rate): -0.35899 
Regressive!

We are to iterate until we beat this. We can:
-Add more data.
-Model in a simpler way.
-Reduce the scope (only worrying about assessed values, and not taxation).
